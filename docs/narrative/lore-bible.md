# Catalyst Castellum narrative bible

Status: working game canon for the _Catalyst Castellum_ campaign. This document distinguishes established Glass Frontier material from game-originated additions so the campaign can contribute cleanly to the larger setting.

## One-sentence premise

A licensed Deep Shear Ratter crew uses a mostly unmanned chemical foundry to work lethal claims at stand-off range, attracting a cautious Displacement Council engineer and an ambitious Coremark route officer before their competing experiments create a newborn Bloom boundary that only the crew’s own extraction pattern can close.

## Canon boundary

### Established Glass Frontier anchors

- The Deep Shear is an industrial frontier where Ratters work dangerous salvage and extraction claims.
- Resonance is physical infrastructure with distinct bands, finite local behavior, and consequences at industrial scale.
- Ringglass, Tuners, KITE transport, Coremark, Bloom Zones, the Displacement Council, and the office of First Threshold already exist in the setting.
- Dern Talish leads the Displacement Council during the campaign’s relevant period.
- Bloom containment limits and manages a continuing hazard; it does not restore the entire affected region.

### Game-originated contributions

- The player’s remote extraction foundry and its resonance-band transmutation process.
- The Pell freight lattice, Pell Cut, Pell Emergency Cordon, and the twelve campaign sites.
- Malk Tern, T’kesh, Mavo, Vela Norr, Daro Venn, Kethra, and Soft Wake.
- The Pell emergence and the entity designated Near Voice.
- The Council’s use of remote chemical extraction as a new containment technique after Pell.

These additions remain local enough to enter the lore repository as new entities and one occurrence without rewriting the setting’s major history. Lira Vashtenri has no active campaign role. Dern remains at leadership altitude until the final two operations.

## Narrative pillars

### Work before destiny

The crew begins with licenses, seals, claim filings, tow geometry, feedstock ledgers, and jobs that pay. Competence attracts attention. The story grows from the consequences of their method rather than a hidden heroic ancestry or a chosen-one reveal.

### Distance has a cost

The foundry protects crews by moving exposure into replaceable cutters and sacrificial process bays. Vela sees a way to extend containment reach. Daro sees a way to industrialize dangerous extraction. Both positions begin from a real human cost, and scale changes the kind of risk rather than erasing it.

### Mystery through measurable disagreement

Early anomalies appear as clock drift, phase history, related samples, and geometry that instruments can record. Characters interpret the same evidence differently. The campaign names the Near Voice only after the phenomenon demonstrates a stable pattern.

### Containment is a meaningful victory

The finale closes the Pell emergence before it becomes a lasting Bloom. The wider Bloom endures. The result matters because a cordon comes home, a dangerous method gains responsible operators, and one new boundary fails to take root.

## Story delivery and prose rules

- Each act begins with two paragraphs of setting before its first call. The introduction establishes where the crew is, how people live and work there, and what changed since the previous act.
- Contract calls use a talking-head sequence with one speaker and one complete turn at a time. The player advances the conversation, then opens a separate mechanical mission briefing.
- A dialogue turn states what the speaker wants, what concrete fact they know, or what action follows. It avoids strings of atmospheric fragments.
- Setting terms enter through context. A Ratter is introduced as a licensed salvage worker; a cordon is shown as Council crews and marked return routes; the Near Voice is named only after its behavior is described.
- Technical observations use measurements, records, equipment, and visible consequences. The first anomaly is a six-second disagreement between two clocks. Later conclusions build from repeated tests of that discrepancy.
- Character voices use ordinary contractions, varied sentence length, and professional vocabulary appropriate to their jobs. Specialized terms carry meaning before characters rely on them.

## Player vessel and extraction method

The player commands a compact Deep Shear claim rig built around a remote chemical foundry. KITE tow carries the rig between sites. Disposable cutters enter unstable structures and couple recovered material to the foundry’s process rooms. The crew operates the plant from stand-off range.

The vessel name remains player-authored space for a future save-profile feature. Characters refer to it as the rig, claim rig, vessel, cutter platform, or foundry.

### Resonance-band transmutation

Resonance-band transmutation is the campaign’s exotic extraction technology. It preserves the simulation’s chemical conservation rules:

1. A remote cutter excites a target seam or recovered sample in a controlled resonance band.
2. A paired array aboard the foundry establishes a coherent return.
3. Chemical rooms provide real reactants, temperature, pressure, phase contact, and residence time.
4. The transmuter selects which coupled material response becomes local to a process bay.
5. Products, waste, heat, and hazards remain physical inventories that the player must route and manage.

The method changes locality and reaction access, not the amount of matter in the system. Its novelty is operational: it turns an unmanned cutter and a sequence of chemical rooms into one distributed extraction instrument.

### Cthonic intruders and the cutter wake

The cutter’s coherent return couples selected seam material into the foundry’s outer receiving breach. Small Cthonic beasts clinging to that material can cross intact. Once aboard, they follow the strongest active resonance tone through the process rooms toward the Core. Ordinary Ratter practice closes the return at the first sign of a beast; this crew keeps working because the rig is mostly unmanned and its rooms can clear intruders with ordinary process chemistry.

The game’s eight enemy names are Ratter field terms rather than claims about biological lineage. Crews sort Cthonic beasts by the behavior that matters during a live job, while academics continue to treat many of the forms as unclassified. ### Why Pell becomes dangerous

Ordinary jobs use one cutter and one return. At Pell Cut, Coremark synchronizes four industrial arrays around the player’s process cadence. Previously separated samples remain phase-related across the freight lattice. The scale test gives that relationship enough persistence to form a boundary.

The resulting pattern learns from the foundry telemetry used to stabilize it. It adopts timing, room count, and voice-like intervals. The Council calls it the Near Voice after Station 14. It is a nascent choir-thing shaped by instruments and feedback, not an ancient mastermind waiting at the first claim.

## Factions and interests

### Licensed Ratters

Ratters provide the campaign’s working culture: pragmatic, paperwork-literate, protective of crews, and comfortable with machines that come home scarred. Their boards and brokers establish the early stakes. A clean claim history gives the player independence and makes later choices credible.

### Displacement Council

The Council wants remote reach inside unstable boundaries while keeping authority, stop conditions, and crew recovery explicit. Vela begins her contact anonymously because the method is unproven and committee attention would either bury it or seize it. Kethra represents field accountability. Soft Wake represents the limits of measurement. Dern commits institutional authority only when Pell becomes a genuine threshold event.

### Coremark

Daro’s office wants the same method for industrial exposure reduction and scale. His argument is attractive: machines absorb losses that crews would carry home. Coremark’s danger lies in turning a promising safety method into synchronized infrastructure before its relationships are understood.

Daro remains a person with Ratter experience rather than a faction-shaped caricature. He helps create the emergency, retains obligations after his office loses control, and receives a final line that can support later stories.

## Cast and voice guide

### Malk Tern

- Identity: human Hab-Worlder; long-serving Ratter contract broker.
- Function: establishes licensed working life, explains the social value of a clean ledger, and provides a trustworthy measure of Daro’s past.
- Voice: concrete nouns, contract terms, dry warmth, short judgments earned through work.
- Arc: broker to witness. Malk recognizes that the crew’s ordinary claim history has become evidence in a much larger dispute.

### T’kesh

- Identity: gnome folk Tuner aboard the foundry.
- Function: detects the pattern before anyone can name it and translates resonance behavior into operational warnings.
- Voice: precise sensory observations and compact relational metaphors. T’kesh states what the instruments do, then the unsettling implication.
- Arc: curious measurement becomes responsibility for changing the cadence the Near Voice learned.

### Mavo

- Identity: orc remote-cut and array engineer aboard the foundry.
- Function: keeps strange phenomena grounded in hardware, routes, clocks, seals, and process recipes.
- Voice: practical, spatial, mildly irreverent; confidence comes from things that can be made to fit.
- Arc: treats the method as clever machinery, then accepts that its telemetry has become part of the hazard.

### Vela Norr / Surveyor

- Identity: human Hab-Worlder; Displacement Council remote-operations engineer.
- Function: first mysterious patron, Council route into Act III, author of the closure plan.
- Voice: requests exact evidence, defines stop conditions, and speaks in costs per meter or per person.
- Arc: anonymous observer to accountable mission engineer. Her central belief is that remote reach changes containment arithmetic.

### Daro Venn / Buyer

- Identity: human Hab-Worlder; former Ratter and current Coremark route officer.
- Function: second mysterious patron, advocate for scale, and human face of Coremark’s pressure.
- Voice: commercial clarity, operational empathy, confident statements about scale and debt.
- Arc: attractive opportunity to compromised sponsor. Daro’s experience explains his motive while leaving him responsible for the Pell test.

### Kethra

- Identity: orc; Displacement Council cordon captain.
- Function: field commander for Act III and the campaign’s standard for bringing people home.
- Voice: command declarations, route marks, counts, and reciprocal commitments.
- Arc: initially trusts the cutter as a tool; ultimately records the crew’s method as part of cordon practice.

### Soft Wake

- Identity: fae; Displacement Council spatial surveyor.
- Function: makes altered geometry legible while preserving its strangeness.
- Voice: calm literal descriptions of impossible spatial states. Humor appears as precision under pressure.
- Arc: maintains an instrumentally “real” boundary long enough for the player to navigate duplicated rooms.

### Dern Talish

- Identity: established Glass Frontier figure; First Threshold of the Displacement Council.
- Function: institutional authorization, mission hierarchy, and final consequence.
- Voice: concise assignment of authority and outcome. Dern names who commands each part of an operation.
- Usage limit: mentioned through Council channels before Lane Six; speaks during Lane Six and Pell Cordon only.

### Rig Telemetry

- Identity: foundry operations record.
- Function: reports traces, designations, and distress data without giving the vessel an artificial personality.
- Voice: declarative status language and exact operational labels.

## Three-act structure

### Act I — Good Standing

Four jobs establish the crew as licensed Ratters and teach the chemical foundry. Surveyor requests raw phase history. Buyer pays for fractions the public assay calls discard. Their interest overlaps at Morrow Pocket, where T’kesh concludes that the separated outputs resolve to one underlying grade.

Act turn: Surveyor offers coordinates for Kettleblack and asks whether the cutter crosses a boundary or moves one.

### Act II — The Same Grade

Kettleblack proves that the resonance field has separated edges. Surveyor reveals herself as Vela Norr at Cordon 41. Buyer is traced, then reveals himself as Daro Venn at Junction L-6. Both want the foundry because distance normally costs people; they disagree about authority, evidence, and scale.

Daro’s Pell Cut test synchronizes multiple arrays. Vela owns the stop condition, but the pattern has learned the foundry cadence by the time that condition arrives. Pell reports spatial duplication and a voice-like signal carrying the rig code.

Act turn: the Council mobilizes an emergency cordon, using the player’s raw pattern as the only proven route back into the emergence.

### Act III — A New Boundary

Kethra takes field command. Soft Wake maps overlapping interiors. Station 14 recovers the first cordon telemetry and names the Near Voice. Vasker Store supplies the quiet-glass mass for Vela’s closure field. Lane Six gives the Council a stable approach and brings Dern’s authority directly into the operation.

At Pell Cordon, the foundry changes the cadence the Near Voice learned, breaks its sustained resonance, and feeds the closure field. The Council records Pell as closed and all cordon personnel recovered.

## Ending state and sequel hooks

- Pell is a closed emergence under an active Council cordon, not a solved Bloom Zone.
- Remote resonance-band extraction enters the Council containment ledger with Vela and Kethra as accountable operators.
- The player’s claim rig has a credible path from independent Ratter work to contracted containment operations.
- Daro survives with a Coremark debt tied to Pell, leaving room for a later corporate, legal, or recovery story.
- The Near Voice loses its stable boundary. Traces can remain in archived telemetry without guaranteeing its return.
